Pipes and Power Expanded adds pipe networks and modular steam machinery. The path into steam runs: water supply => boiler => engine => sub-machine. This article covers the pipes everything breathes through; see Steam Power: Boilers, Steam Power: Engines and Steam Power: Fittings for the machines.

How pipe networks work
Connected pipes form one network with a shared pool. A network carries one medium at a time — a gas (air, blast, exhaust, steam) or water, never both. Each pipe adds 30 L of capacity; pressure is simply volume over capacity, shown in atm. The whole network also shares one temperature.

Materials matter. An iron pipe bursts above 5 atm, a steel pipe above 10 atm — and the weakest pipe limits the whole run. An over-pressured run holds for about 30 seconds, then a pipe lets go.

Open ends leak. Any unconnected pipe connector bleeds the network (≈8 L/s of gas, 10 L/s of water), so a leaking run can never build pressure. Cap runs against machine ports, valves or outlets. Standing water in pipes and boilers also slowly evaporates (≈50 L per day).

Building runs. Straights, bends, T- and X-junctions snap their orientation to neighbouring pipes when placed; rotate a piece with a wrench if it guessed wrong. Pipes connect to machines through their port faces (boiler outlets, engine inlets, pump and intake flanges) — butt the pipe straight against the port.
