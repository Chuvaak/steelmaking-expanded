Engines take steam from a pipe network and drive one attached sub-machine. Place the engine first (it raises through right-click construction stages, like boilers), then place its sub-machine in the marked cell beside it — it snaps into the right facing on its own.

Watt Engine — the low-pressure starter.
- Runs at 2-4 atm inlet steam; above 4 atm it wears toward a break (~60 s grace).
- Draws 30 L/s of steam, delivers 0.3 power, and dribbles ~1 L/s of hot condensate out its drain — pipe it away or it spills on the floor.
- Construction stages consume: 66 x Metal Plate, 42 x Metal Rod, 14 x Metal Nails & Strips.

Cornish Engine — the high-pressure workhorse.
- Runs at 6-8 atm. Its control rods set the working rate: Low / Normal / High draw 8 / 16 / 32 L/s of steam and deliver 0.5 / 1 / 2 power.
- Construction stages consume: 46 x Metal Plate, 42 x Metal Rod, 14 x Metal Nails & Strips.

Sub-machines (one per engine, placed in the engine's drive cell):
- MP Generator — turns axles. It is a constant-power source: within the engine's rated load it holds normal speed; pile on more machines and the whole network slows; past roughly double the rated load it overstresses and stops. A Watt engine comfortably runs a couple of helve hammers; a Cornish engine on High runs a whole workshop.
- Fluid Pump — transfers water from its intake line into its output line, about 5 L/s at full engine power. Its output is pressurised to ~75% of the engine's inlet steam pressure — enough to push water up into a boiler.
- Cornish Air Blower (from Steelmaking Expanded) — pressurises air into Blast for the blast furnace and Bessemer converter.

Breakage and repair. An engine run above its pressure band for too long breaks: it stops and leaks steam. Look at it while holding a wrench to see the repair bill, then rightmouse with the materials in your hotbar to fix it. Mind the Cornish-boiler-into-Watt-engine trap: that boiler can push 5 atm, the Watt breaks at 4 — regulate with a pressure valve.
